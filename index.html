2005

For many years, the contribution of the Chinese railway workers went largely uncelebrated. On May 27, 2005, Canadian Pacific Railway named the railway interchange in Kamloops, British Columbia after Chinese labourer Cheng Ging Butt. The Cheng Interchange honors the many labourers who toiled, some sacrificing their lives, to build the western section of the CPR from Port Moody to Craigellachie, BC.

1889

The railway extended from coast to coast reaching Saint John, NB, and the CPR was expanding into other businesses.

1885

The company had run out of money and needed more to finish building the tracks. They proved to the Canadian government the usefullness of the railway and the Canadian government decided to help the CPR with their financial problems. Just a few months later, on November 7, 1885, Donald A. Smith drove the last spike into the railway tracks at Craigellachie, BC.

1882

With Van Horne in charge of construction, the railway was almost finished now. There was a problem of how to get through the mountains. Major A.B. Rogers found a possible route and the problem was solved.

1881

The Canadian government declared the Pacific Railway company "official". The company was given $25 million and 25 million acres of land to build a railway. At first, things didn't go well. Then, the company hired William Cornelius Van Horne. His job was to finish building the railway over the Prairies and through the mountains.

1871

British Columbia decided to join Canada, but only if the Canadian government promised to build a transcontinental railway. British Columbia set a 10 year deadline for the completion of this link to the rest of the country.
